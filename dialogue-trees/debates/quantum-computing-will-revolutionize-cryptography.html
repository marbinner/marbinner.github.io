Quantum computing will revolutionize cryptography

Background & Definitions

Background

Quantum computers (QCs), particularly through the theoretical efficacies of Shor’s and Grover’s algorithms, pose an existential threat to the foundational public-key cryptography schemes (like RSA and ECC) used globally for secure data transmission and digital signatures. The debate centers not on the mathematical possibility of this threat, but on the practical timeline for the development of a cryptographically relevant quantum computer and the scale of the resulting disruption to global digital infrastructure.

Key Definitions

Revolutionize
To usher in a fundamental and comprehensive alteration in the principles, methods, and established practices within the field of secure communication.
Cryptographically Relevant Quantum Computer (CRQC)
A robust quantum machine with sufficient stable qubits and low error rates capable of executing Shor’s algorithm efficiently enough to break current standard public-key cryptosystems, such as 2048-bit RSA.

✓
Objection: The claim ignores that widespread global integration (TTI) often takes 10-15 years, especially across legacy infrastructure and deeply embedded systems, which may significantly exceed the unknown and potentially rapid time-to-quantum-threat (TTQT).

Objection: The transition may be revolutionary regardless of timing because the large key and signature sizes of PQC candidates like Dilithium and Falcon inherently demand costly redesigns of memory-constrained hardware and communication protocols.

✗
Quantum computing’s threat is limited primarily to public-key systems (like RSA and ECC) via Shor's algorithm; widely used symmetric-key cryptography (e.g., AES-256) only faces a quadratic speed-up from Grover’s algorithm, which is easily mitigated by doubling the key size.
▶
✓
Objection: Quantum computers can break the collision resistance of cryptographic hash functions (like SHA-256) at a complexity of $O(2^{n/3})$ or $O(2^{n/4})$, not just the $O(2^{n/2})$ speed-up against symmetric keys. This greater relative reduction in security means the quantum threat is not limited primarily to public-key systems alone.

✗
Building fault-tolerant, scalable quantum computers capable of breaking current cryptographic standards requires millions of stable, high-fidelity qubits, a severe engineering challenge that pushes the relevant timeline decades into the future, enabling a proactive and gradual defense migration.

✓
Objection: Exponential advances in qubit fidelity and error correction, such as Qiskit's roadmap anticipating 1,000 logical qubits by 2025, contradict a guaranteed multi-decade buffer; sudden breakthroughs could compress the timeline to less than 10 years, demanding an accelerated PQC migration.

✗
The immense scale and embedded nature of the current Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) across global networks and billions of devices prevent any sudden "revolution." Replacing such deeply rooted infrastructure will necessarily be a slow, expensive, multi-decade process of crypto-agility.

✓
Objection: Rapid shifts can be forced by regulatory mandates or critical external factors, significantly compressing timelines. For instance, the mandated transition from analog to digital television in the U.S. was completed in just five years despite the massive infrastructural scope.

Privacy is more important than national security

Background & Definitions

Background

This debate addresses the fundamental conflict between the collective need for safety and the individual right to seclusion and control over personal information, especially in the context of modern mass electronic surveillance. The tension is heightened by technological advancements that allow governments to collect vast amounts of data, leading civil liberties advocates to question the necessity and proportionality of security measures that infringe upon personal freedom. The core issue is determining which value should take precedence when they come into direct opposition.

Key Definitions

Privacy
The right of an individual to control the collection, use, and disclosure of their personal information and to be free from unwarranted governmental intrusion.
National Security
The protection and maintenance of the stability of the state, its institutions, and its citizens from large-scale internal or external threats such as terrorism, espionage, and cyberwarfare.
Privacy Technology Policy
Proposition: Privacy is more important than national security

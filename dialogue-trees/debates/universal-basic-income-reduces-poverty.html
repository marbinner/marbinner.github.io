Universal basic income reduces poverty

Background & Definitions

Background

Universal Basic Income (UBI) is widely discussed as a potential policy response to mitigate rising economic inequality, job displacement due to automation, and chronic poverty across nations. Proponents argue that unconditional cash transfers provide a secure financial floor ensuring basic human needs are met and stabilizing communities. Opponents question its immense fiscal feasibility and potential negative effects on inflation, the labor supply, or social motivation. The core disagreement centers on whether UBI is the most effective and sustainable mechanism for achieving substantial poverty reduction.

Key Definitions

Universal Basic Income (UBI)
A governmental program providing recurring cash payments delivered to all individual residents or citizens without means testing or work requirements.
Poverty
The condition of an individual or household lacking the financial resources to meet a designated minimum standard of living, frequently measured using absolute income thresholds or relative measures tied to a national median income.
Reduces
The debate concerns whether the implementation of UBI causes a statistically significant and sustained decrease in the number of people falling below established poverty lines or if it increases the economic well-being of the poorest segment of the population.
